The $500k Blank Check: Why Minors Shouldn't Be Direct Beneficiaries

Back to Insights

You did the exact right thing: You purchased a life insurance policy to ensure that if the worst happens, your kids are financially protected. You filled out the paperwork, wrote their names on the beneficiary line, and crossed it off your to-do list.

But by putting their names directly on that line, you may have accidentally created a massive financial and legal headache for the people left behind.

The Hidden Danger of Minor Beneficiaries

Here is the reality of the insurance industry: The insurance company cannot pay proceeds directly to a minor.

If you pass away and your 10-year-old child is listed as the direct beneficiary, they will require a court-appointed guardian, custodian, or trust to be established before releasing the funds. Because minors cannot legally own large amounts of property or manage finances, the state courts are forced to step in.

"Life insurance isn't just about picking a number; it's about making sure the money actually protects your family the way you intend."

In many cases, if a minor is named directly, the court will require a guardian or conservator of the estate to be appointed to manage the funds—even if a surviving parent is present. That process can involve court oversight, annual reporting, and administrative costs every step of the way.

The 18-Year-Old Blank Check

The potential court oversight is frustrating, but it isn't the biggest risk. The real danger happens down the road.

In most cases, once the child reaches the age of majority (typically 18 or 21, depending on your state), the remaining balance is turned over to them outright.

Imagine a $500,000, $750,000, or $1,000,000 lump sum deposited directly into a high school senior's checking account. No restrictions, no rules, no oversight. Even the most responsible 18-year-old is rarely equipped to manage half a million dollars meant to last a lifetime.

How to Fix It

The good news is that this is entirely preventable with the right planning. You need a combination of the right insurance policy and the right legal structure.

Instead of naming your minor children directly, many experts recommend naming a Trust or setting up specific custodial accounts as the beneficiary. By doing this, you can maintain control from beyond the grave, dictating exactly how and when the money is distributed.

For example, a trust can stipulate that the funds be used for health, education, and living expenses while they are young, and then release the remaining principal in stages—perhaps 25% at age 25, 25% at age 30, and the remainder at age 35.

Please Note: Because laws vary by state, it is important to coordinate beneficiary decisions with a qualified estate planning attorney when setting up trusts or legal structures.
