The Million-Dollar Mistake: Why Your Kids Shouldn't Be Your Beneficiaries

Back to Insights

You did the exact right thing: You purchased a life insurance policy to ensure that if the worst happens, your kids are financially protected. You filled out the paperwork, wrote their names on the beneficiary line, and crossed it off your to-do list. It feels like the ultimate act of protection.

But in reality, by listing their names directly on that line, you have likely built a legal wall between your children and the money they need to survive. It is one of the most common—and most expensive—mistakes in estate planning.

The Trap of State-Mandated Management

Here is the hard truth of the insurance industry: Insurance companies are legally prohibited from paying large sums of money directly to minors.

If you pass away and your 10-year-old is the direct beneficiary, the insurance company will freeze those funds. They will not release a single dollar until the state court appoints a guardian or conservator to manage the "estate." This is true even if there is a surviving parent. The court essentially takes control of the money you meant for your family, requiring expensive annual reporting and legal oversight—all paid for out of your children's inheritance.

"Life insurance is about providing a future, not a legal battle. Naming a minor directly is essentially handing the keys to a judge."

The "18-Year-Old Financial Free-for-all"

While the court oversight is a headache, the real danger is the "automatic payout." In Wisconsin and most other states, the moment that child turns 18, the court's job is done. The entire balance of that life insurance policy—whether it’s $250,000 or $1,000,000—is handed over to them in one lump sum.

Most 18-year-olds are not equipped to manage a life-changing amount of wealth. Without a structure in place, that money, intended to fund a decade of education and a head-start in life, is often exhausted within a few short years on depreciating assets and short-term spending. You lose the ability to guide them from beyond the grave.

The Solution: Planning Beyond the Policy

This is entirely preventable. The goal is to make sure the money is available for their needs (health, education, maintenance) without giving them total control before they have the maturity to handle it.

Instead of naming your children, you should consult with an estate planning attorney about naming a Trust as the beneficiary. A trust allows you to name a person you trust (a Trustee) to manage the money and dictates exactly when the kids get access—perhaps 1/3 at age 25, 1/3 at 30, and the rest at 35. This ensures the money lasts for its intended purpose: their long-term security.
