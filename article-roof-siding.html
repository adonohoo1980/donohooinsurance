The Two Hidden Traps in Roof and Siding Claims

Back to Insights

A major Midwestern hail storm rolls through your neighborhood. The next day, you see roofing contractors knocking on doors up and down the street. Your neighbor gets a brand new roof and new siding, and they tell you, "It only cost me my $1,000 deductible!"

You call your insurance company, expecting the same smooth process. But instead of a full replacement, you receive a check for $4,000, a bill from the roofer for $15,000, and an adjuster telling you they are only replacing one side of your house's siding.

What went wrong? You likely fell victim to one of two common, hidden clauses often buried in cut-rate home insurance policies to keep premiums artificially low.

Trap #1: The Actual Cash Value (ACV) Roof

When it comes to property insurance, there are generally two ways a policy can value your roof: Replacement Cost Value (RCV) or Actual Cash Value (ACV).

A policy with Replacement Cost is what most homeowners assume they have. If a storm destroys your roof, the insurance company pays the current market rate to tear it off and install a brand new one (minus your deductible), regardless of how old the damaged roof was.

However, many online or deeply discounted policies sneak in an Actual Cash Value roof endorsement. ACV means the insurance company factor in depreciation based on the age and wear of your roof.

"If your roof was designed to last 30 years and the storm hits in year 15, an ACV policy may only pay you for half the cost of a new roof. You are responsible for the rest."

That means the $20 a month you "saved" on your premium could end up costing you $10,000 out of pocket when it comes time to replace the shingles.

Trap #2: The Siding Matching Nightmare

The second major trap often catches homeowners completely by surprise. Imagine a fallen tree branch heavily damages the vinyl siding on the east side of your home. The other three sides are perfectly fine.

The insurance company correctly agrees to pay to replace the damaged east side. But there is a massive problem: your siding color was discontinued by the manufacturer seven years ago. The new siding they put up won't match the rest of your house, leaving your home looking like a patchwork quilt.

Does the insurance company have to replace the three undamaged sides just so everything matches?

The answer heavily depends on your specific policy language. While Wisconsin courts have weighed in on "matching" disputes over the years, many standard policies state they will only pay to replace the materials that were physically damaged. Unless your policy includes a specific Matching of Undamaged Siding/Roofing Endorsement, you could be stuck with a mismatched house—or forced to pay out of pocket to reside the rest of the home.
